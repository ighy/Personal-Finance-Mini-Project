# Insights
### Cash Flow Analysis
Monthly net cash flow is consistently positive, with a single exception in March, which recorded a net loss of *199 BYT*. This negative month is isolated, indicating no persistent imbalance between income and expenses.

From October onward, the data shows noticeable declines in remaining account balance, signaling the onset of a higher-spending period. This pattern is consistent with seasonal expense pressure, such as end-of-year obligations or discretionary holiday spending, rather than a structural change in income.

Despite short-term volatility, the long-term trend remains stable and positive across the full 11-month period. While early-month analysis may overemphasize the March deficit, a full-period view confirms that this loss does not persist or compound, and therefore does not represent a sustained cash-flow risk.

There is no evidence of a structural imbalance between inflows and outflows. When evaluated holistically, the account demonstrates cash flow sustainability rather than financial stress.

### Account Health
* Cash flow is highly concentrated, with Account 1 generating the majority of net inflows and accounting for most expense outflows, making it the primary driver of overall liquidity.

* Account 2 captures a meaningful share of income but supports relatively low expenses, indicating a secondary operating or transfer role.

* Accounts 4 and 5 receive small portions of income and exhibit minimal spending, consistent with savings or emergency reserve accounts.

* Account 3 is structurally unprofitable, recording approximately 64 USD in income against 1,354 USD in expenses, and therefore requires ongoing subsidization from other accounts, most likely Accounts 1 and 2.

* Overall account health is maintained through cross-account subsidization and role specialization, introducing concentration risk due to heavy reliance on Accounts 1 and 2.
